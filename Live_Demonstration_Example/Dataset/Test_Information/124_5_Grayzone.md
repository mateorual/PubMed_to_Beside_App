## Left Lung Ultracentral, Radioresistant Metastasis: A Double Dilemma

Jonathan B. Wallach, MD, and David L. Schwartz, MD 

Department of Radiation Oncology, Veterans Affairs New York Harbor Healthcare System - Brooklyn Division, State University of New York - Downstate Medical Center, Brooklyn, New York 

Received Aug 6, 2025; Accepted for publication Sep 5, 2025 

A 73-year-old man with coronary artery disease, type 2 diabetes mellitus, obesity class 3, and oligometastatic transverse colon adenocarcinoma in remission off systemic therapy for 5 years, underwent a surveillance fluorodeoxyglucose ([1] ⁸F) positron emission tomography/computed tomography. Imaging demonstrated a 2.1 cm mass straddling the distal left mainstem bronchus and proximal left upper lobar bronchus, deemed ultracentral owing to abutment of the left mainstem bronchus, and no other lesions (Fig. 1).[1] Its medial border was 2.75 cm from the trachea and 3.10 cm from the esophagus, measuring 14.0 cm[3] . He had no prior radiation therapy, and his systemic therapy did not include vascular endothelial growth factor inhibitors. An endobronchial ultrasound/biopsy demonstrated tumor cells positive for CK20/CDX-2 and negative for CK7/TTF-1, consistent with colon adenocarcinoma, an extremely radioresistant metastatic histology to the lung.[2] Owing to the lesion’s ultracentral location and the patient’s comorbidities, thoracic surgery did not recommend resection.

2. Would the treatment incorporate systemic therapy? 

3. What risks of high-grade toxicity would you discuss with the patient? 

The patient tolerated 60 Gy/12 fractions volumetric modulated arc therapy very well, without any acute, subacute, or chronic side effects. However, 3 years later, he experienced progressive hemoptysis. A positron emission tomography/computed tomography demonstrated a hypermetabolic mass superolateral to the original site (Fig. 2). ## Recovery at the Edge of the Limits 

Our initial management for this ultracentral oligometastasis[1] would have been hypofractionated radiation therapy, 60 Gy in 15 fractions (as per the LUSTRE trial[2] ), using 4- dimensional computed tomography (4DCT) simulation with intravenous contrast, respiratory gating if motion > 7 mm, a 5-mm planning target volume (PTV) margin without a clinical target volume, volumetric modulated arc therapy (VMAT) planning, and 4D cone beam CT scan for matching. No systemic therapy would be incorporated. We would quote a 5-10% risk of grade ≥ 3 toxicity and a 1-2% treatment-related mortality risk, extrapolating from the SUNSET trial.[3] The second CT simulation would be in the same position to facilitate registration, with fusion of the PET-CT scan to inform target delineation. Dose accumulation would be performed using deformable registration and with calculation of equivalent doses in 2 Gy fractions (EQD2). Before planning, our medical physicist would provide quality assurance on all deformations and fusions and create avoidance structures for the dosimetrist in areas of

We anticipate that the inferior portion of the new plan will overlap with the superior portion of the prior plan, requiring a compromise of target coverage to respect organ-at-risk constraints, particularly for the tracheobronchial tree and esophagus (see Table 1 for estimated allowable doses). We would attempt a dose of 60 Gy in 8 daily fractions using VMAT, using SUNSET trial constraints for the new plan, but we expect the inferior parts of the GTV and PTV to receive less. 

There is very little data on appropriate accumulated doses and recovery factors. Reirradiation is akin to walking a tightrope: either too much or too little dose can cause morbidity and death, the former because of harm from radiation and the latter because of harm from tumor growth. We would enroll this patient in our REPAIR trial (NCT06558175; currently allowing 33% tissue recovery after 36 months); therefore, the previous dose would be weighted at 0.67 in the composite with an accumulated dose limit to bronchus D0.1cc ≤ 80 EQD2. If a safe plan cannot be generated with reasonable coverage (PTV D95 > 80% prescription dose), we would de-escalate to 60 Gy in 15 fractions or, as a last resort, to 45 Gy in 15 fractions.

## What Should be the Initial Treatment? 

Because no “standard” radiation dose/fractionation exists for ultracentral lung tumors, a hypofractionated radiation (HYPORT) approach rather than classic Stereotactic Body Radiation Therapy (SBRT) seems prudent because of the proximity of large airways.[1] I prefer 4 Gy £ 15 fractions to a total of 60 Gy, extrapolating from the LUSTRE trial[2] for primary lung tumors (including central and ultracentral), with a 3-year local control of 87.6% (SBRT) versus 81.2% (HYPO-RT). 

Treatment volumes would include a 6-mm expansion to the Planning Target Volume (PTV) from the 4-dimensional-simulated Internal Gross Target Volume (I-GTV), based on the fusion of the computed tomography and positron emission tomography images. Contouring the airways

## How to Treat This Isolated Recurrence? 

Assume that hemoptysis is due to tumor recurrence rather than late toxicity and that the atelectasis is due to the obstructing tumor rather than the bronchial stenosis. Delivering radiation seems the only effective option. 

The recurrent tumor seems likely separated from the initial tumor and away from the large airways, but extreme care is needed to create a good composite plan following a 4-dimensional simulation, trimming the margin of PTV if necessary and prioritizing the avoidance of any airway overlap over the PTV coverage. Daily image guidance will be cone beam computed tomography (4-dimensional cone beam computed tomography if available). If no PTV overlap exists, again 4 Gy £ 15 fractions may be used. Otherwise, 2.5 Gy £ 20 to 50 Gy seems safer.

## When Toxicity Exceeds Benefit: A Case of Thoracic Reirradiation 

**==> picture [29 x 29] intentionally omitted <==**

Discussions regarding the intent of local therapy in the setting of metastatic disease are nuanced and require the input of the patient in shared decision-making.[1] In my practice, patients have shown a variable threshold for risk in ultracentral, isolated, inoperable metastatic disease. I use a hypofractionated approach for upfront management of patients like this, especially in radioresistant histology. Excellent performance status is a dual indication for both definitive local therapy and maintenance systemic therapy. Given the micrometastatic environment that will exist after local therapy, I refer for ongoing use of systemic therapy. 

Reirradiation with definitive intent has been shown to be safe and effective in carefully selected patients who have isolated recurrence of primary and metastatic disease.[2] The intent of reirradiation depends on the equivalent dose in 2 Gy fractions (EQD2) calculation from prior radiation therapy. In this patient, the EQD2 to the airway that directly abuts the tumor (a/b of 3) is 96, given prior doses of radiation therapy (60 Gy in 12 fractions). I do not offer definitive dosed reirradiation when the composite EQD2 exceeds reirradiation guidelines (EQD2 of 110 Gy for the airway).[3] Additionally, the patient’s presentation with hemoptysis can be an indication of airway erosion by tumor. High-dose reirradiation to airways with eroded mucosa exceeds an acceptable risk and places the patient at risk for massive hemoptysis and death. For these reasons, I recommend endobronchial debulking followed by a quad shot (3.5 Gy twice a day £ 4 fractions). If performance status allows, I refer for ongoing use of systemic therapy.
